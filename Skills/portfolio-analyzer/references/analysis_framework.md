# Portfolio Analysis Framework

This document provides comprehensive frameworks for analyzing investment portfolios and generating strategic insights.

## Analysis Dimensions

Every portfolio analysis should consider these key dimensions to provide complete insights:

### 1. Asset Allocation Analysis

Examine how the portfolio is divided across different investment types:

**Equity vs Fixed Income vs Cash**: What percentage is in stocks versus bonds versus cash equivalents? This reveals the risk profile. Aggressive portfolios might be 90%+ equities, while conservative portfolios favor bonds and cash.

**Market Capitalization Mix**: How much is in large-cap versus mid-cap versus small-cap stocks? Large-caps offer stability, small-caps offer growth potential but with higher volatility.

**Geographic Diversification**: What percentage is domestic versus international? Concentration in one geography (especially home country) creates regional risk. A well-diversified portfolio typically includes 20-40% international exposure.

**Sector Allocation**: Which economic sectors dominate the portfolio (technology, healthcare, financial services, etc.)? Sector concentration creates risk if that sector underperforms.

### 2. Concentration and Diversification Metrics

**Position Sizing**: 
- Red flag: Any single position exceeding 20% of total portfolio value
- Caution: Top 5 positions exceeding 60% of portfolio
- Ideal: No single position above 10%, top 10 positions under 50%

**Number of Holdings**:
- Too few (<10): Insufficient diversification, high individual stock risk
- Sweet spot (15-30): Manageable and well-diversified
- Too many (>50): Over-diversification, difficult to monitor, increased complexity

**Correlation Risk**: Are the holdings truly diverse, or do they move together? Tech stocks might seem diverse but often correlate highly with each other.

### 3. Investment Strategy Identification

Identify what strategy the portfolio reflects:

**Growth Strategy**: High concentration in technology, healthcare innovation, emerging sectors. Low dividend yields, high P/E ratios. Focuses on capital appreciation.

**Value Strategy**: Investments in undervalued companies with low P/E ratios, high book value. Often includes mature industries, financial services, energy.

**Income Strategy**: High dividend-paying stocks, bonds, REITs. Lower growth potential but steady income generation.

**Index/Passive Strategy**: Holdings mirror major indices (S&P 500, Total Market). Low fees, market returns.

**Hybrid/Balanced**: Mix of growth and value, income and appreciation goals.

### 4. Risk Assessment

**Market Risk (Beta)**: How sensitive is the portfolio to overall market movements? High-beta portfolios amplify market swings.

**Volatility**: What's the expected price fluctuation? High-growth portfolios typically experience higher volatility.

**Concentration Risk**: Over-weighting in specific stocks, sectors, or asset classes.

**Liquidity Risk**: Can positions be easily sold without significant price impact? Small-cap and international stocks may have lower liquidity.

**Currency Risk**: For international holdings, exchange rate fluctuations create additional risk.

### 5. Performance Context

**Time Period Matters**: Recent performance should be viewed across multiple timeframes (1 month, 3 months, 1 year, 3 years, 5 years) to distinguish luck from skill.

**Risk-Adjusted Returns**: A 20% return with 40% volatility may be worse than a 15% return with 10% volatility. Consider return per unit of risk.

**Benchmark Comparison**: Performance means nothing without context. Compare against:
- Relevant index (S&P 500 for US large-cap, Russell 2000 for small-cap)
- Blended benchmark matching the asset allocation
- Peer portfolios with similar strategies

## Strategic Recommendation Framework

When making recommendations, structure them across these categories:

### Immediate Concerns (Address First)

Issues requiring prompt attention:
- Excessive concentration creating outsized risk
- Poor diversification leaving portfolio vulnerable
- Holdings misaligned with stated risk tolerance
- Tax inefficiencies that can be corrected

### Rebalancing Opportunities

Situations where the portfolio has drifted from target allocation:
- Asset classes that have grown too large due to performance
- Underweighted areas that deserve increased allocation
- Harvesting gains to reduce concentration

### Strategic Adjustments

Longer-term portfolio evolution recommendations:
- Sector exposure changes based on market trends
- Geographic allocation shifts
- Moving from active to passive (or vice versa)
- Adjusting risk profile as circumstances change

### Enhancement Opportunities

Ways to improve portfolio efficiency:
- Reducing fees through lower-cost alternatives
- Improving tax efficiency
- Adding uncorrelated assets
- Filling gaps in diversification

## Common Portfolio Concerns Checklist

When asked "do you have concerns with my portfolio," evaluate these systematically:

**Concentration Issues**:
- Is any single position above 15% of portfolio?
- Do the top 5 holdings exceed 50% of portfolio?
- Is there excessive sector concentration?

**Diversification Gaps**:
- Are there major asset classes missing (bonds, international, real estate)?
- Is geographic diversification adequate?
- Are holdings truly uncorrelated?

**Risk Alignment**:
- Does the risk level match the investor's stated tolerance?
- Is the portfolio appropriate for the time horizon?
- Are there hidden correlations creating more risk than apparent?

**Cost Efficiency**:
- Are there high-fee funds that could be replaced with low-cost alternatives?
- Is there unnecessary overlap between holdings?

**Tax Considerations**:
- Are tax-inefficient holdings in taxable accounts?
- Are there tax-loss harvesting opportunities?
- Is portfolio turnover creating unnecessary tax events?

**Market Timing Risks**:
- Is the portfolio overly concentrated in recent hot sectors?
- Does it reflect recency bias (buying what's been working)?
- Are fundamentals supporting current valuations?

## Comparing to Market Trends

When asked to "compare investment strategy to market trends":

1. **Identify Current Market Regime**: Is the market favoring growth or value? Are we in a risk-on or risk-off environment? What sectors are leading?

2. **Assess Portfolio Positioning**: How is the portfolio positioned relative to these trends? Is it aligned with or contrarian to current trends?

3. **Evaluate Appropriateness**: Being contrarian isn't inherently badâ€”sometimes the best opportunities come from going against the crowd. Assess whether the positioning is intentional strategy or accidental drift.

4. **Consider Time Horizon**: Short-term misalignment with trends may not matter for long-term investors. Distinguish between trading and investing.

5. **Identify Opportunities**: Where could the portfolio benefit from exposure to emerging trends? Where might it be overexposed to fading trends?

## Monthly Review Structure

For monthly portfolio reviews, follow this sequence:

1. **Performance Summary**: How did the portfolio perform this month? Compare to relevant benchmarks.

2. **Attribution Analysis**: What drove performance? Which holdings contributed most? Were there any significant detractors?

3. **Allocation Check**: Has the allocation drifted from targets? Do any positions need rebalancing?

4. **Risk Assessment**: Have any new risks emerged? Has concentration increased?

5. **Market Context**: What happened in markets this month? How did it affect the portfolio?

6. **Action Items**: What changes, if any, should be made? Prioritize by importance.

7. **Forward Look**: What should be monitored in the coming month?
